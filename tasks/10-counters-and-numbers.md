## What is missing

**Numbers** have no lesson anywhere.

**Counters** are the bigger gap. In Japanese you cannot say "three books" with a
bare number: you attach a word that depends on what you are counting.

- 三**枚** — three flat things (paper, plates, shirts)
- 三**本** — three long things (pens, bottles, umbrellas)
- 三**匹** — three small animals
- 三**台** — three machines or vehicles
- 三**人** — three people

There is no way to guess which one a noun takes; it has to be learned per
category. There are dozens in common use, and a handful cover most daily life.

## What the audit found

The counter kanji 本, 枚, 匹, 台 **appear in the app only as kanji meanings**. So a
learner reaches the point of being able to read 三枚 and not know what it means,
having been taught both characters separately and never told what they do
together.

The Japanese auditor named this as one of the gaps that caps the reading ceiling
below what the kanji count suggests.

## Scope to decide

1. **Numbers first.** 1-10 have irregular readings (一つ ひとつ vs 一 いち), and
   4/7/9 have two readings each with rules about which to use. That alone is a
   lesson.
2. **Then the common counters**, as a set rather than one at a time, since the
   whole idea is that the counter varies with the thing.
3. **Where it lives.** This is neither a kanji lesson nor a word lesson nor a
   grammar pattern. It may want its own small track, or a place in the
   writing-rules shelf, or to sit with grammar. Worth deciding before building.

## Done when

- Numbers are taught, including the irregular readings.
- The common counters are taught as a set.
- A learner meeting 三枚 knows what it means.
